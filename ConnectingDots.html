Methods for adapting a product
to an international market

Various approaches have developed for designing products for a vast international market. This is a sampling of terms, which refer to various ways of approaching cultural diversity in product design:

Standardization of technology has the advantage of improved compatibility, but poses usability problems for certain ethnic groups to whom the metaphors and visualization used in the interface are foreign (Shen). Furthermore, standardization threatens a loss of cultural identity and tradition, which could result in globalization, a theory that Marshall McLuhan spoke of in 1967 in which digital media would result in a collective perspective of the world shared by humanity as a whole. Globalization seeks cultural compatibility at the cost of ethnic diversity. However, it is also argued that mass media communication and information distribution are introducing diversity to previously isolated and homogenous societies.

Internationalized products are designed to be culturally androgynous for potential use anywhere. Microsoft and other international technology companies pair this with localization, which is the process adjusting the product to the appropriate cultural context for the user. In addition to language, time zones, currency, conceptual models, layout, color, and icons may be adjusted to maximize personalization for and communication with the user, while not impacting the original program and development process (Microsoft).

In The Lexus and the Olive Tree, Thomas Friedman defines glocalization as “the ability of a culture, when it encounters other cultures, to absorb influences that naturally fit into and can enrich that culture, to resist those things that are truly alien and to compartmentalize those things that, while different, can nevertheless be enjoyed and celebrated as different” (Friedman).

Culturalization has emerged in resistance to globalization by placing value and emphasis on preserving cultural traditions. It is a movement that calls people to reject the digital revolution in favor of their identity and heritage. This trend suggests that a product developed from within a culture will be more successful with locals than a global product adapted to target that culture (Shen).
