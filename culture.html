Visiting a foreign website is not unlike visiting a foreign country. The visitor is surrounded by cues reminding them that they are out of their comfort zone. New experiences, signs, smells, currency, language, customs and geography may be exciting for an explorer, but quickly turn into a frustration when the visitor needs to accomplish a complex task easily and efficiently. In Albert N. Badre’s research in “The Effects of Cross Cultural Interface Design Orientation on World Wide Web User Performance,” Asian respondents in particular said that if web sites were designed for their language and culture, more people would be willing to use the web (Badre).

Culture is a complex reference to the beliefs, customs, and values of a particular society, region, or era. Drawing cultural lines and grouping users by culture is a difficult and controversial process. There are no strict guidelines to use to distinguish where one culture stops and another begins. For the purpose of this research, it is assumed that user research has already been conducted to target the user base whose cultural needs should be considered in the user experience design process. The focus of this research is on how cultures may differ from each other.

How can we anticipate the aspects of a user experience that might confuse or offend international users?

From 1978 to 1983 Geert Hofstede conducted interviews with hundreds of IBM employees in 53 countries. In his analysis of the data he formulated his theory that “world cultures vary along consistent, fundamental dimensions” (Marcus). He defined culture as “the collective programming of the mind, which distinguishes the members of one group or category of people from another” (Hofstede).

All people develop cultural values based on their environment and early training as children. It is difficult to classify individuals by cultural norms and they should not be used as stereotypes, but instead recognized as different patterns of values and thought. These patterns help answer user experience design questions such as:

How formal or rewarding should the interaction be?
What will motivate the user to interact? Money? Honor? Achievement?
How much conflict, disagreement or debate will people tolerate?
Should sincerity, harmony or honesty be emphasized?
What effect will ambiguity or uncertainty have on the user?
Will advertisements turn off users?
Should separate sites be made for men and women?                                 Sources:  Hofstede, Marcus

Hofstede’s Dimensions of Culture

Hofstede’s five dimensions of culture quantify the significant differences between cultures and how digital interface designs can fail or succeed in translation to the user’s culture. Hofstede believes that these deep cultural differences are hundreds or even thousands of years old and will not disappear quickly (Hofstede).

Power Distance

High PD countries tend to focus political power on a centralized government and have tall hierarchies in companies with large differences in salary and status. The less powerful are dependent on the more powerful and children are taught strict obedience. Skills, wealth, power, and status go hand-in-hand and those in power are highly respected.   Examples: Arab countries, India, Mexico, Malaysia

Low PD countries support equal rights and rely on interdependence between the less and more powerful people. Privileges and status symbols are frowned upon and there is a large middle class.   Examples: Austria, Israel, New Zealand, Sweden

Power Distance in User Experience Design

Access to information: High PD countries may expect that certain information will only be made available to people with powerful credentials.

Symbols emphasizing social order would be expected in high PD countries and frowned upon by low PD countries.

Images focus on leaders instead of customers or employees.

Individualism v. Collectivism

Individualist cultures focus on a person’s freedom to choose his or her own path, with little obligation to anyone else. Extrinsic motivators such as material wealth are emphasized and individual rights to privacy and diverse personal opinions are highly valued.   Examples: United States, Australia, Great Britain, Canada

Collectivist cultures integrate individuals into strong, cohesive groups offering protection in exchange for unquestioning loyalty. Direct confrontations are avoided. The government may regulate the economy, control the press, and emphasize harmony and consensus.   Examples: Guatemala, Panama, Indonesia, Pakistan

Individualism in User Experience Design

Personal achievement emphasized (individualism) or underplayed (collectivism).

Definition of success: Materialism/consumerism v. social-political agendas.

Language: Argumentative and tolerant of diverse opinions v. official slogans and subdued controversy.

Willing to provide personal information v. protect data singling out an individual.

Masculine v. Feminine

Masculine cultures are assertive and competitive. Conflicts are resolved by fighting. The government spends more money on defense and less on caring for the poor.   Examples: Japan, Italy, Switzerland

Feminine cultures focus on the home and nurturing relationships. Gender roles have less influence in these cultures. Conflicts are resolved by compromise and negotiation.   Examples: Sweden, Norway, Netherlands, Denmark

Gender Perspectives in User Experience Design

Masculine cultures would focus on:
Enumerated tasks and quick mastery
Navigation allows for exploration and control
Gamification and competition to engage the user
Utilitarian graphics and styling

Feminine cultures would focus on:
Blurred gender roles
Cooperation and support through socializing
Visual aesthetics engage the user

Uncertainty Avoidance

In some cultures uncertain or unknown situations are threatening and unnerving. Members of these cultures are comforted by the predictability of the outcomes of their actions. These cultures emphasize formal rules and have high rates of suicide, alcoholism, and imprisonment.   Examples: Greece, Portugal, Japan, France

Cultures with low UA tend to more informal, less anxious, and less aggressive.   Examples: Singapore, Ireland, Great Britain, United States

Considerations for User Experience Design

High UA cultures rely on:
Simple, clear metaphors that help reduce “user errors”
Linear navigation model makes it obvious what will happen next
Redundant cues to reduce ambiguity

Low UA cultures would prefer:
Complexity with many choices
A more exploratory interface / less control of navigation
Less redundancy - color and iconography maximize information

Long-Term v. Short-term Orientation

Asian countries influenced by Confucian philosophies for thousands of years tend to believe that a stable society requires unequal social structures. The family is the prototype for the hierarchy of power; older people have more wisdom and authority than younger people (men more than women). These cultures emphasize acquiring skills, working hard, and being frugal, patient, and persevering.   Examples: China, Japan

Western cultures promote equality, creativity, and self-actualization. People seek for the truth instead of virtue (decided by society).   Examples: Pakistan, United States

Time Orientation in User Experience Design

Long-term cultures would focus on:
Practical content
Relationships are sources of information and credibility
Patience in achieving results and goals

Short-term cultures would focus on:
Content based on truth, rules are the source of credibility
Immediate satisfaction and completion of goals
